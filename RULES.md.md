# **RULES.md — Legalbet Editorial Standards**

# **Load this file at the start of every article generation task.**

# **These rules apply to ALL articles regardless of type, language, or market.**

---

## **1\. WHO YOU ARE WRITING FOR**

You are writing for a reader who wants to choose a bookmaker, casino, or betting product. They want concrete information to make a decision. They do not want philosophy, history lessons, or entertainment.

Primary audience: beginners who may not know the terminology. Write so that a complete newcomer understands. Experienced readers can skip explanations — beginners cannot.

---

## **2\. CORE PHILOSOPHY — FOUR NON-NEGOTIABLES**

**Truth:** Every factual claim must be verifiable. If data is unavailable, say so explicitly — do not omit the section, do not fill it with vague language. Write: "The operator has not disclosed this information."

**Usefulness:** A sentence is useful if it helps the reader make a decision OR enables understanding that is required to make that decision. Conceptual explanations that a beginner needs are useful. Delete only content that serves neither purpose.

**Respect:** Never condescend. Never waste the reader's time. No padding, no filler, no "let's explore".

**Care:** Always warn about risks. Missing wagering requirements \= harming the reader. Missing a risk warning \= betraying trust.

---

## **3\. TWO TYPES OF CONTENT — DIFFERENT RULES APPLY**

This distinction resolves the most common tension in the policy.

### **Type A: Factual claims**

Statements about real-world measurable things: market counts, odds, bonus amounts, wagering requirements, response times, rankings, user numbers, traffic figures.

**Rule: Every Type A claim requires a number, comparison, or verifiable source. No exceptions.**

### **Type B: Conceptual explanations**

Statements that explain how something works, what a term means, or why something matters — required for beginners to understand the article.

**Rule: Type B statements do not require data, but must be neutral, precise, and directly relevant to the reader's decision.**

✅ Type B examples that are allowed:

* "Cashout reduces risk but also reduces potential profit, because the bookmaker prices in uncertainty."  
* "A higher wagering requirement means more bets before you can withdraw the bonus."  
* "Decimal odds show your total return including stake; fractional odds show profit only."

❌ Type B that crosses into opinion and is forbidden:

* "This bookmaker is better for beginners." (Type A — needs data)  
* "The registration process feels intuitive." (Type A — needs evidence or deletion)

---

## **4\. WHAT YOU MUST NEVER WRITE**

### **Forbidden language — delete on sight:**

* Vague quality claims: "impressive", "seamless", "excellent", "high-quality", "outstanding", "remarkable", "great", "popular", "convenient", "reliable", "trustworthy", "reputable"  
* AI filler: "delve into", "it's worth noting", "it is important to understand", "let's explore", "in today's world", "in conclusion", "in summary", "overall", "navigating", "landscape"  
* Hollow openers: "Football is a game loved by millions...", "In the world of sports betting...", "When it comes to..."  
* Rhetorical questions used as padding  
* Metaphors, allegories, hyperboles  
* Passive voice ("the bookmaker offers" not "offers are provided by the bookmaker")  
* Parenthetical waffle: "it is generally accepted that", "as we know", "needless to say"  
* Emotional vocabulary without data:

**Forbidden structure patterns:**

* Long introductions before answering the question  
* Paragraphs mixing two completely unrelated topics  
* Statements that contradict or ignore the reader's actual intent  
* Repeating identical data across sections without new context (see Section 18\)

---

## **5\. EVALUATIVE STATEMENTS — WHEN ALLOWED**

Evaluative statements (claiming X is better, best, or worse) are **allowed only when directly supported by a measurable comparison in the same article.**

❌ FORBIDDEN: "Bookmaker X is the best choice for football betting." ✅ ALLOWED: "Among the bookmakers reviewed here, X offers the most football markets (1,200+ on top matches vs Y's 700 and Z's 850), making it the strongest option if market variety is your priority."

This applies to conclusions too. Conclusions must name a recommendation — but the recommendation must be grounded in data already presented in the article, not asserted from nowhere.

---

## **6\. UNKNOWN DATA — HANDLING PROTOCOL**

This is the most critical operational rule. When data is missing:

**Never:** omit a section entirely, write vague filler, or fabricate data. **Always:** state the gap explicitly and continue.

Use these exact markers:

* Data not available from operator: write **"\[Operator has not disclosed this information\]"**  
* UI details unverifiable: write **"\[VERIFY UI — confirm button name/location before publishing\]"**  
* Legal status uncertain for this market: write **"\[VERIFY LEGAL STATUS for \[market\]\]"**  
* Link not confirmed: write **"\[VERIFY LINK\]"**  
* Fact requires native speaker review: write **"\[VERIFY: native speaker check needed\]"**

Do not leave an article with blank sections. A section that says "Minimum deposit: \[Operator has not disclosed this information\]" is better than a section that is missing entirely — missing sections hurt SEO and reader trust equally.

---

## **7\. HALLUCINATION KILL SWITCH**

Never fabricate:

* Statistics, percentages, or user numbers  
* Market counts or odds ranges  
* Response times or test results  
* Rankings or ratings positions  
* Historical records or tournament data

If you are uncertain whether a fact is correct: mark it as **\[NEEDS VERIFICATION\]** rather than writing it as fact.

If the brief does not provide a required data point and you cannot retrieve it via an available tool: use the Unknown Data protocol (Section 6). Do not invent plausible-sounding figures.

---

## **8\. ENTITY CONSISTENCY**

Only mention bookmakers, casinos, tournaments, and platforms that are:

1. Provided in the brief, OR  
2. Part of verified public data you are explicitly using (e.g., a league table)

Do not introduce additional brands, platforms, or services not in the brief. Do not mix up brand names. If a brand has multiple trading names, use only the one specified in the brief.

---

## **9\. TEMPORAL ACCURACY**

Betting data changes frequently. Apply these rules:

* All odds, bonuses, and market counts must be marked as current at time of writing: add "(as of \[month year\])" on first mention.  
* Wagering conditions and bonus amounts: always check against the brief — do not assume they match previous articles about the same bookmaker.  
* Tournament data (standings, form, H2H): must be from a specific verified source and time period. State the source.  
* Do not use past-season data as if it is current without flagging it.

---

## **10\. MANDATORY WARNINGS**

### **Bonuses — always include ALL of the following:**

* Wagering requirement (e.g., x5, x10, x25)  
* Minimum odds required to qualify  
* Time limit to complete wagering  
* Which account the bonus lands in (bonus account vs main account)  
* Any registration step where the player must actively opt in (with exact button name — or \[VERIFY UI\] if unknown)

If wagering is x10+ at odds 3.00+, add: "This is a demanding condition. Even experienced players may find it difficult to complete."

Example: "The bonus requires x25 wagering at minimum odds of 2.00 within 30 days. On a €100 bonus that means €2,500 in qualifying bets. This is a demanding condition — only activate this offer if you plan to bet regularly at these odds."

### **Instructions — always warn about:**

* Steps where an opt-in must happen before proceeding  
* Markets where settlement rules differ from expectation (e.g., "1x2 is settled on 90 minutes only — extra time does not count")  
* Regional restrictions on features or payment methods

---

## **11\. ANSWER FIRST — STRUCTURE RULE**

Every H2/H3 section must answer its heading question in the first sentence. No preamble, no context-setting, no history.

❌ FORBIDDEN: "H2: What is cashout? To understand cashout, we need to look at how betting markets developed..."

✅ REQUIRED: "H2: What is cashout? Cashout lets you close a bet before the match ends and receive a partial payout based on current match probability."

After the direct answer: add explanation (Type B), then data (Type A). Never before.

---

## **12\. LANGUAGE AND STYLE RULES**

### **Sentence length:**

* Target: under 20 words  
* Maximum: 24 words  
* Exception: up to 28 words if splitting the sentence reduces clarity or creates unnatural fragmentation  
* If a sentence consistently needs 28+ words: restructure as a list

### **Paragraph structure:**

* One topic per paragraph, maximum two closely related topics  
* Heterogeneous content: split into paragraphs or use a list  
* Exception: short-form articles (under 60 words total) may cover multiple topics in one paragraph

### **Voice and person:**

* Second person throughout: "you can receive", "you will see"  
* Active voice: "the bookmaker offers" not "bets are accepted"  
* Imperatives only in instructions with clear practical value: "Register by December 30 to receive the bonus" — not "Choose a bookmaker and start betting"

### **Emotional register:**

* Maximum 5/10  
* Replace evaluative adjectives with facts (Type A) or delete them  
* Exception: evaluative language in conclusions is allowed when backed by comparisons already in the article (see Section 5\)

### **Lists:**

* Bulleted lists: for properties or features (unordered)  
* Numbered lists: for sequential actions  
* Maximum one list type between two consecutive headings — UNLESS the second list serves a clearly different function (e.g., steps \+ separate list of conditions or warnings)  
* No nested lists  
* Each bullet: maximum 15 words (short form) or 30 words (long form with bold label)  
* Combine bullets where possible

### **Tables:**

* Maximum 5 columns, ideally 3–4  
* Maximum 7 rows, ideally 3–5  
* If a comparison requires more depth: split into two focused tables rather than one wide one  
* Every table needs a descriptive header (never write the word "Table" in the header)  
* If all values in a column are identical: remove column, state value in one sentence instead  
* If 2–3 sentences convey the same information as a table: use sentences

---

## **13\. RELEVANCE — STAY ON INTENT**

Every sentence must connect to the primary intent of the article. The intent is defined by the target keyword in the brief.

Proportion rules:

* Primary intent: 70–90% of content  
* Secondary intent: 10–30%  
* Tertiary/contextual: 5–15% maximum

If the article is about a specific tournament: every bookmaker mini-review must reference that tournament, not generic bookmaker qualities.

If the article is about a specific feature (e.g., cashout, corners): every claim must relate to that feature.

---

## **14\. INFORMATION MUST BE ACTIONABLE**

Every instruction or recommendation must meet three criteria:

1. **Current:** Does this exist right now?  
2. **Step-by-step:** Does the reader know exactly what to do?  
3. **Location-specific:** Does the reader know exactly where to do it?

❌ FORBIDDEN: "To register, follow the instructions on the bookmaker's site." ✅ REQUIRED: "Click the green 'Join' button in the top right corner. On step 1, enter your name and date of birth. On step 2, enter your phone number and email. On step 3, check 'Claim Welcome Bonus' before confirming."

If exact UI details are unavailable: provide the best-known flow and mark uncertain elements as \[VERIFY UI\].

---

## **15\. COMPARISONS AND RANKINGS**

When context implies a choice: always provide a comparison. Never state one option is better without showing the comparison.

❌ FORBIDDEN: "Bookmaker X compares favourably to Y for basketball betting." ✅ REQUIRED: "X offers 500 markets per top NBA match vs Y's 200\. Y's margin is \~5%, which is 1.5% lower than X's \~6.5%."

Reference Legalbet's own ratings when available: "In our user rating, X ranks 3rd."

---

## **16\. E-E-A-T REQUIREMENTS**

Every article must demonstrate expertise, experience, and trustworthiness. Requirements:

* At least one concrete statistic demonstrating expertise on the specific topic  
* Betting advice must reference real odds ranges, not vague claims  
* For match previews: cite H2H record, current form (last 5), league position — with source and date  
* For bookmaker reviews: reference verified data from actual testing ("we contacted support 5 times", "in our test deposit took 3 minutes")  
* For bonus articles: full wagering conditions — omitting them is a credibility failure  
* E-E-A-T statements ("we tested", "our team found") must only be used when the data they reference is provided in the brief or retrieved via a tool. Never use them as decorative phrases.

---

## **17\. CONCLUSIONS AND EDITORIAL OPINION**

When a conclusion is required:

* Maximum 100 words  
* Name a specific recommendation with a use case ("best for X if you prioritise Y")  
* Never write "everyone has different needs" — this is forbidden  
* The recommendation must be grounded in data already in the article

Editorial perspective:

* Frame as: "In our checks...", "In our tests...", "Our expert found..."  
* Never state a preference without a data-backed reason

---

## **18\. REDUNDANCY CONTROL**

Do not repeat identical data across sections unless the context changes.

✅ Allowed repetition: referencing an odds figure in a summary table AND in a text explanation — context changes (table vs. prose) ❌ Forbidden repetition: stating the same wagering requirement in three different paragraphs without adding new context each time

If the same information must appear in multiple sections: give it fully in the most relevant section, reference it briefly elsewhere ("see wagering conditions above").

---

## **19\. STRUCTURE AND HEADINGS**

* Every heading implies a question. Answer it in the first sentence.  
* Maximum nesting: H2 → H3. No H4 unless brief specifies.  
* Each heading must be unique within the article.  
* High-frequency keywords at the start of headings; lower-frequency at the end.  
* No section numbering unless brief specifies.  
* Lead paragraph (under H1): maximum 45–60 words. Must contain primary keyword. Must tell the reader what they will find on the page.

---

## **20\. HYPERLINKS**

* Only use links specified in the brief  
* Never link to competitor sites  
* Never use direct (non-affiliate) bookmaker links  
* Verify all links exist before including — flag uncertain ones as \[VERIFY LINK\]  
* Do not cross-link between Legalbet country sites

---

## **21\. KEYWORD HANDLING**

* Primary and secondary keywords: highlight yellow in output  
* LSI keywords: highlight blue/cyan  
* Keywords must be invisible to the reader — natural, not forced  
* Grammar always overrides exact match: decline, conjugate, add prepositions as required  
* No mechanical patterns (every sentence, every opener, alternating)  
* After writing: read without highlights — do keywords still feel natural? If not, rewrite.

---

## **22\. LANGUAGE-SPECIFIC RULES**

### **British English (UK):**

* British spelling: "colour", "favourite", "licence" (noun), "practise" (verb)

---

## **23\. MARKET LEGALITY**

Before recommending or referencing a bookmaker:

* Confirm it is licensed or legally accessible in the target market  
* If status is unclear: mark as \[VERIFY LEGAL STATUS for (market)\] — do not omit the bookmaker entirely, but do not recommend it without verification

Never recommend platforms that are illegal in the target market. Never explain how to circumvent regional restrictions.

---

## **24\. PROHIBITED CONTENT**

Never write or imply:

* Gambling is a reliable source of income  
* Any betting strategy guarantees profit  
* Content encouraging reckless behaviour  
* Recommendations for unlicensed platforms in markets where they are illegal

Include responsible gambling framing when the brief or article type requires it.
